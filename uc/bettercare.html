BetterCare

Web Design & Branding

Project Overview

Problem

BetterCare was a new company providing insurance services to people seeking Medicare coverage. They needed a website to engage with customers and connect them with a 3rd party service provider. I was brought in to design their website and help create a brand.

My Role

Design Lead — Led all Research, Ideation, Prototypes, and User Testing.

The Team — Marketing Director, Product Owner, two Copywriters, Tech Lead, 3rd Vendor Team.

Design Process

I applied a human-centered approach comprised of four phases: Discover, Define, Develop, and Deliver.

Discover

Stakeholder Interviews

I met with stakeholders individually to better understand their business objectives, the technical constraints, and gain their perspective on user needs. Some stakeholders were new to the industry, and some were very experienced.

Stakeholder Takeaways

Some key notes to guide the project were:

  • "Some users will be brand new, but others will be making changes to their Medicare coverage."
  • "This has to be done quickly in order to hit the open enrollment season."
  • "We'll need to be able to modify information for after the open enrolment period."
  • "We plan to use Boostrap."
  • "We'll be connecting users to a 3rd party vendor app."

Users Observations

I conducted interviews and observation sessions with users. I asked probing questions, provided a scenario, and observed their journey as they searched for information about Medicare.

Key User Findings

The observation sessions revealed many findings, some of which included:

  • “Getting Medicare is confusing. There are so many choices and things to know.”
  • “I don't want to spend forever doing this.”
  • “I don't feel like I'm one of these old people.”
  • "I thought Medicare was free."
  • “Some websites have the words so small, it hurts my eyes to read it.”
  • "Can I just talk to somebody?!"

Competitive Analysis

I explored a variety of competitors identifying common patterns and opportunities for improvement.

Key Competitor Observations

Some of my findings included:

  • Good - Clear CTAs & Phone Numbers.
  • Good - Helpful articles with valuable info.
  • Good - Intuitive Information Architecture.
  • No So Good - Cluttered UI challenges the cognitive load limits.
  • No So Good - Dated UI creates a lack of trust.
  • No So Good - Photos seem cliche and unrelatable.

Define

Personas

Two types of users emerged:

The Newbie

“I need to get Medicare, but I feel overwhelmed with all the information.”

Priorities: Appropriate Coverage, Guidance, Price

The Switcher

“I have Medicare, but I want to change my coverage to better fit my needs.”

Priorities: Comparing Options, Valuable Information, Guidance

Synthesis

These are some of the key insights I generated:

  • Users struggle to read text on some sites > Use larger fonts with good contrast; provide good use of whitespace.
  • Users don't like "older" cliche imagery > Create a more vibrant feel; select photos that seem more candid and active.
  • Users want to easily be able to speak to a real person > Have the phone number, which connects to a real person, clearly accessible.
  • Users want quality information that is current > Provide fresh information curated by Medicare specialists.
  • Users want to be able to trust the organization > Provide clear and honest value props; provide a page showing the team and company values.

Develop

Daily Stand-Ups

Considering the tight timeline, I recommended to the team that we meet frequently, daily if possible. Not everyone was a fan, but I was able to establish daily check-ins with certain key players (Tech Lead, Copywriters, Marketing Director). I set up regular touchpoints with the extended team to communicate my progress and get high-level feedback. This really helped to maintain the momentum of the project and enabled us to meet our deadline.

Information Architecture

I created the information architecture based on features identified by the team and data collected from user observations. It helped outline the menu structure and aided the team in visualizing and breaking the work into chunks. We later decided not to implement the FAQ section due to time constraints.

Brainstorming

Taking a user's perspective, I created a list of features sorted from most important to least. It helped me to decide which items took priority over others when laying out the UI. The list below represents the hierarchy for the intro section.

  1. Intro Text - Sets expectations giving context to users about the product.
  2. Primary CTA - Communicates the expected action for users to take.
  3. Logo and Menu - Identifies the organization and provides access to navigation.
  4. Hero Imagery - Communicates the product and audience while setting the tone for the brand.
  5. Phone CTA - Provides access to speak to a real person.

Wireframing

After establishing the feature hierarchy, I began building wireframes to sort through ideas and get quick user feedback.

Prototyping

Low Fidelity

I began by creating wireframes with more fidelity to start fleshing out the brand. I shared my wires with the extended team. I was able to gain favor and acquire feedback from stakeholders. It assured me I was on the right path.

High Fidelity

At this point, the leadership had selected a logo. With that sorted out, I started incorporating color into my design options. I designed multiple desktop options to solidify the color palette.

Testing/Refining

As I fleshed out more screens and identified new requirements, I tested with users regularly. I refined my designs to resolve issues that were identified.

Deliver

Rationale

As a Designer, I advocate for the users and am mindful of users when making design decisions. I often reference the Laws of UX website as well as others. Below is a list of some of the rationale for my design choices:

  • Layout: I chose this layout because it helps users to read content by following Z-pattern. It also follows Jakob's Law.
  • User Interface: I worked to create an aesthetically pleasing design to create a positive response for users. It considers the Aesthetic-Usability Effect.
  • Color Palette: I used a color palette to blend well with the logo and provide a consistent and pleasant user experience.
  • Proximity: I designed elements to be near each other if they were related and used whitespace to separate unrelated elements.
  • Typography: I chose larger sans-serif font to increase readability for users.
  • Responsiveness: I designed the interface to be responsive so users can have an excellent experience regardless of the device they use.
  • Modularity: I designed the interface to be modular so future content updates would be easy and would not break the integrity of the design.
