BetterCare

Branding & Web Design

Overview

BetterCare was a new organization specializing in helping seniors acquire Medicare. They needed a website to connect with their audience. The goal of the site was to help people by providing valuable content, giving a corporate overview, and connecting users with a 3rd party application.

My Role

Design Lead — Led all Research, Ideation, Prototypes, and User Testing.

The Team — Marketing Director, Product Owner, two Copywriters, Tech Lead.

The Process

BetterCare was a new organization without a process defined for building a website. Leading with a clear human-centered process helped define the business vision and kept the project moving forward. I applied a human-centered approach comprised of four phases: Discover, Define, Develop, and Deliver.

The Problem

Seniors can get overwhelmed with choosing the right Medicare coverage for their needs. BetterCare seeks to build a website to connect seniors with Medicare specialists that help them select their best coverage.

Research

Empathize with Users

I interviewed seniors to understand how they'd look for Medicare coverage and what challenges they've faced with other websites.

The overarching finding was that users felt the process took too long. My research provided insight into why users felt negative about the experience.

15 possible screens
88 possible questions
9.6 minutes average

Most people felt the process should take 5 minutes at the most.

Defining User Types

Our research revealed two different types of user:

The Newbie

“I'm researching Medicare and don't know how to get the right coverage. I also don't want to overpay.”

Priorities: Appropriate Coverage, Guidance, Price

The Switcher

“I have Medicare, but I want adjust my coverage to better fit my needs.”

Priorities: Comparing Options, Valuable Information, Guidance

Identifying User Pain Points

Research had revealed many key user pain points, some of which included:

  • “Getting insurance is confusing. There are so many choices and things to know.”
  • “I don't want to spend a lot of time doing this.”
  • “I do not feel fully confident figuring it out on my own.”
  • “Some websites have the text so small that it hurts my eyes to read.”

Ideation

Human-Centered Design

Empathy map exercise that was conducted with stakeholders.

I conducted design-thinking exercises with stakeholders including Business, Member Service Representative, and Design partners to share an understanding of the issues, ideate on solutions, and build consensus on the direction.

The Big Idea

After synthesizing our research data, one key technical insight stood out:

“All we need is a VIN to quote another vehicle.”
- Product Owner

With this in mind, I explored starting our app by asking only the essential vehicle questions, then prefilling the rest of the quote values based on information gathered from the user's existing policy.

We went from asking 88 potential questions to asking as few as 3 to view a quote.

After receiving the prefilled quote, users could then verify the information for accuracy with the ability to edit changes in context.

Concept Design

In exploring the existing information architecture, I knew that our app had multiple entry points. This led me to consider a modular type of interface that could be accessed from anywhere on usaa.com. Now that our app had as few as three questions, I considered a chat-based UI as a solution.
